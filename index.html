Why This Matters

The homes between the landmarks matter too.

Chicago's historic neighborhoods are defined not only by landmark buildings, but by the many individual homes that together create a distinctive architectural fabric. Each year, historic residences are lost to demolition, redevelopment, or significant alteration.

The Chicago Historic Plaque Program is designed to help homeowners and neighbors better understand the history of these properties — and recognize the importance of preserving them.

This pilot is expected to begin in selected historic areas of Lincoln Park, including the Sheffield Historic District and nearby historic residential blocks.

The Plaque

A dignified marker with a timeless format.

Each plaque is a cast bronze oval designed to be mounted on a building or displayed on a freestanding stake — a permanent recognition of the home's place in Chicago's history.

Eligibility

Who may qualify for the pilot program.

The pilot is expected to focus on homes with documented historic or architectural significance. Final eligibility standards will be set by Preservation Chicago as the program develops. Potential factors include:

Age

The age of the home connects it to architectural eras and significant events, and influences its historical context.

Architectural character

Properties that retain original or notable architectural features contributing to the historic streetscape.

Historic district location

Location within or near a recognized historic district, such as the Sheffield Historic District in Lincoln Park.

Historic association

Connection to a notable original owner, builder, architect, or documented historic use of the property.

Please note: Participation in the Chicago Historic Plaque Program does not mean that a property is landmarked, listed on a historic registry, protected from demolition, or eligible for any tax benefit. The program is intended for education, recognition, and preservation awareness only.

How It Works

From inquiry to installation.

The program is expected to follow these steps. Details remain subject to Preservation Chicago review as the pilot develops.

1
Homeowner inquiry

A homeowner expresses interest through this page or by contacting Preservation Chicago directly.

2
Initial review

Preservation Chicago or a designated research assistant conducts an initial review to determine whether the property appears to qualify.

3
Research and verification

Basic historical information is researched or reviewed — including construction date, original owner or builder, and relevant historical context.

4
Plaque text approval

Proposed plaque wording is reviewed by Preservation Chicago before production to ensure consistency and accuracy.

5
Ordering and payment

The homeowner receives final cost information and payment instructions. Payment structure is still being determined.

6
Production and delivery

Once approved and paid for, the plaque is produced and shipped to the homeowner or designated address.

7
Installation

Installation is expected to be handled by the homeowner. Mounting guidance will be provided.

Cost: Final pricing has not yet been determined. The total may include the plaque, mounting option, shipping, taxes, and a modest research or administrative fee. Preservation Chicago will confirm the cost structure before the pilot launches.
